Music therapy for learning disorders



The only South Island-based certified practitioner of a special music therapy is aiming to help Southland children and adults improve their learning, language and social skills.

Te Anau early childhood educator Pam Mitchell trained in the Tomatis Method last year because she wanted to help a friend’s child who was struggling to learn.
“I discovered Tomatis when I was investigating ways to help a friend’s son who had various undiagnosed issues that were hampering his learning. I was amazed by the results.”
In a therapy session, patients listen to songs by Mozart and Gregorian chants that are intentionally altered so the listener's brain is unable to anticipate sudden changes in tonal contrasts. The result, according to the method, is that the brain is 'surprised', thus encouraging it to ‘wake up’ and stimulate the ear and the brain. The Tomatis Method is designed to aid those with learning and language disorders, attention disorders, emotional disorders and motor disorders. It stresses that it is an educational programme, not a medical treatment nor a means to establish a medical diagnosis.

The Rotary Club of Fiordland has funded therapy sessions for fiveyear- old Oliver Salter, who has a severe developmental delay caused by a rare partial chromosome deletion. Oliver's mother Rachel Walker said she thought that there was nothing to lose but potentially a lot to gain when she first heard about the therapy.

“We noticed a change with Oliver’s speech in the first couple of days, he started making new sounds. His behaviour when he is in a social setting has changed a lot, he is communicating more and has a better understanding of his environment,” she said.
Seeing how much the therapy helped Oliver was the catalyst for Mrs Mitchell to resign from her role as an adviser and co-ordinator for home-based childcare company Busy Bodies and become a fulltime practitioner of the method. She could work with people in Southland and Otago and was looking to set up a programme in a school.
Mrs Mitchell said although the therapy had been proven to help children and adults with special needs, it could help anyone of any age improve their concentration, speech and listening skills. “It can help adults and children who have trouble concentrating, who suffer from anxiety, who worry a lot and have co-ordination issues,” she said.
Mrs Mitchell said a standard session consisted of listening to this music via wireless headphones for two hours a day for 13 days in a row. A standard package included three lots of these 13-day sessions with a month's break between each. Mrs Mitchell said she was planning on receiving additional training on how to cater time frames to meet different needs.
The Tomatis listening therapy, which has been available in New Zealand for two years, is used around the globe, including in 200 schools in Europe.

To Whom it may concern,
We had the pleasure of having Pam Mitchell enter our family’s life in 2013, when we were looking for childcare for our two children, William and Jessie. At the time Pam was the visiting teacher for home based childcarers Busy Bodies in Te Anau.
Our son William at the time was 3 and diagnosed with a severe communication disorder and possible ADHD. He required special supervision and had multiple extra services visit him, such as an occupational therapist, speech therapist and early intervention coordinator, while all under the care of a pediatrician. With the help of these services, his home educator Becky, and the guidance of Pam, William started to make some progress.

During 2015, doctors felt that William was exhibiting more markers of ASD (Autism) than previous tests had shown. As William started school in June, Pam approached my husband, Kris, and I about a new therapy that she had researched and trained in. This was Tomatis, a music therapy that was geared to awakening the brain and making new cognitive connections. I considered this alternative medicine, until I spoke with our GP and she said music therapy and physical therapy were some of the only medically proven therapies, that didn’t involve drugs, that helped children on the spectrum.

William began the Tomatis programme during his second term at school. I didn’t think we would make it through one session as William didn’t like things on his head, around his ears or loud music, these things could throw him into a meltdown. I was amazed one day when i arrived to pick him up that he was sound asleep. Within days William had stopped bed wetting, previous visits to an incontinence nurse hadn’t stopped this. He was now sleeping through the night regularly, for the first time in 5 years. He was generally calmer and had longer periods of focus. William’s speech therapist was astounded by the huge advancement in his oral language in the month between visits. After our second round of Tomatis, she felt his oral language skills were now on par with his peers. After round two, changes weren’t as dramatic, but William was focusing in class better, but still achieving “below’ the national standard. Three months later after our 3rd round of Tomatis, William was now achieving “at” the national standard.

As a trained teacher, I have seen the struggles that many children have with school. The fact that William was now achieving, meant he could further concentrate on developing his social skills and continue to be a happy soul.

Our main goal for William has always been to be raise someone who can be happy in themselves while also positively contributing to society. Tomatis has been one of the non-invasive things that has allowed us to set along this path. The care, patience and passion for this therapy and her clients that Pam gives is another serious contributing factor.

I highly recommend this therapy and Pam Mitchell, to all ASD parents, as we all know there isn’t anything to lose from our children listening to beautiful, peaceful music everyday. You will be amazed at the positive changes it will make to your whole family.
Raewyn Harrison

BEd (Tchg), NZDipBus, tired Mum of 2, Wife and Teacher.
